The correct structure

Uber prices in the 11¢.
Then donates from revenue.

The 11¢ is not a surcharge added at checkout. It’s part of the price. Uber collects it as revenue, then donates 10¢ to certified charities through DimeTech.

Why this works: Because the 11¢ is Uber’s revenue (not a pass-through), Uber is donating its own money. Under CRA rules, corporations can deduct charitable donations from taxable income — up to 75% of net income.

The smart approach

Price increase first.
DimeAid second.

During the stealth development phase (6 months), Uber raises prices by 11¢. By the time DimeAid launches, the revenue is already baked in. Zero net cost.

1
Month 0: Uber adjusts pricing by 11¢
Normal price adjustment. Customers don’t notice 11¢ on a $20 order.
2
Months 1–6: Stealth development & testing
DimeTech integration, charity partnerships, compliance setup. Revenue baseline established.
3
Month 6: DimeAid launches with media campaign
Uber announces: “We’re donating from every transaction to end hunger.” The 11¢ is already in the price — no new charge, no backlash.

Result: By launch day, the price increase is old news. The donation is new news. Uber gets credit for generosity that’s already funded. Net cost to Uber: ~0.3¢ per transaction.

Addressing the concerns

Three questions, three answers.

“Will companies share their transactional data?”
DimeTech does not access customer data. We only receive the transaction total and verify the donation flow. No names, no addresses, no payment details. We’re a verification layer, not a data partner.
“Is Uber really saving, or just claiming what they collected?”
With the price-first structure, the 11¢ is Uber’s revenue. Uber donates its own money. The deduction is real. If it were a pass-through customer donation, Uber would get nothing — that’s why the structure matters.
“People hate fees. This will incense them.”
This is not a fee. It’s not a surcharge. It’s not on the receipt as a separate line. It’s built into the price — like every other cost Uber absorbs. The customer sees $18.84, not $18.73 + 11¢. The story is “Uber donates from every transaction” — positive, not extractive.

Canadian consumer protection law requires disclosure of surcharges. Because this is a price adjustment (not a surcharge), standard pricing rules apply. Quebec bans surcharges entirely — this structure avoids that issue.
